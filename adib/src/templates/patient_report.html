What is Pharmacogenetics?

Pharmacogenetics studies how your genes affect your response to medications. Variations in certain genes can influence how your body processes drugs, which may affect how well a medication works or whether you experience side effects.

Understanding the symbols:

▲ Action Needed — Important finding. Talk to your healthcare provider.
◆ Review — Possible variation detected. Discuss with your doctor.
✓ Normal — Standard response expected. No changes typically needed.
— No Data — Could not be determined from available genetic data.

Glossary

Gene
A segment of DNA that contains instructions for making a protein. Certain genes produce enzymes that break down medications.
Allele
A variant form of a gene. You inherit one allele from each parent.
Phenotype
The predicted functional outcome – for example, whether you metabolize a drug normally, slowly, or quickly.
Poor Metabolizer
Your body processes the drug more slowly than average. You may need a lower dose or an alternative medication.
Normal Metabolizer
Your body processes the drug at a typical rate. Standard dosing usually applies.
Rapid / Ultrarapid Metabolizer
Your body processes the drug faster than average. The standard dose may be less effective.
Intermediate Metabolizer
Your body processes the drug somewhat slower than average. A dose adjustment may be helpful.
CYP Enzyme
Cytochrome P450 enzymes – the body's main drug-metabolizing system, found primarily in the liver.
HLA
Human leukocyte antigen – immune system markers that can predict severe drug hypersensitivity reactions.
CPIC
Clinical Pharmacogenetics Implementation Consortium – publishes evidence-based guidelines on using pharmacogenetic test results.
